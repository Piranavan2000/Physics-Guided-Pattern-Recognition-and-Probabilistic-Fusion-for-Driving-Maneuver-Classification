\abstract{
\textbf{Problem Statement:} Purely data-driven maneuver recognition models often suffer from a \textit{Sim-to-Real Generalization Collapse}. While the AI model achieves near-perfect recall (99.94\%) on specific benchmarks, this performance often reflects a high degree of convergence on domain-specific spectral noise signatures and sensor sampling biases (e.g., Motorola XT1058 hardware profiles). This convergence introduces a critical \textit{stochastic noise sensitivity}, where performance collapses (28.61\%) when deployed in cross-domain simulator environments.
\textbf{Methods:} We implement a model-agnostic probabilistic soft fusion layer that integrates domain-specific physical constraints at inference time. The system utilizes a Weighted Ensemble (RF, XGBoost, LightGBM) trained on a 60/30/10 fusion of real-world and CARLA simulator data, gently fused with Newtonian kinematic priors ($\alpha = 0.1$).
\textbf{Experimental Protocol:} External validation was conducted using the Mendeley Dataset (Nazirkar, 2021), CARLA, and Synthetic benchmarks. Statistical stability was verified over 10 independent iterations.
\textbf{Results:} The proposed framework achieved a Mean Macro-F1 of $0.7224$ ($\sigma = 0.0050$). Critically, while the AI-Only model exhibited overconfident hallucinations in the CARLA domain (28.61\%), the Hybrid model effectively bridged the gap, raising recall to 74.71\%. The results demonstrate that soft fusion preserves precision (0.98) while filtering high-frequency road noise.
\textbf{Conclusion:} By treating physics as a "Generalization Safety Net," the system resolves the safety-accuracy paradox, ensuring robust maneuver recognition across unseen sensor hardware.
}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Modern Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) frameworks are shifting away from infrastructure and toward vehicle-based telemetry, however this new trend results in the model receiving erratic signals from consumer mobile sensors. According to the WHO, escalating fatality rates have pushed the industry toward proactive monitoring systems on a global scale \cite{who2023}. At a localized level, data from Sri Lanka (2002-2019) highlights specific behavioral causes: overtaking (16.5\%), diversion (14.5\%), speed driving (13.3\%) \& alcohol consumption of the driver (4.5\%) were prominently noted, while 'others' comprised 48\%. Thus, Road Traffic Accidents (RTAs) are subject to the influence of several distinct human factors \cite{epid2024}. We propose to build automated systems to detect these hazardous driving patterns such as sharp braking, lane changes, and swerving before they cause accidents.

We use smartphone-grade IMUs to collect road safety data, enabling large scale crowd-sourcing of road safety data \cite{handel2014}. Smartphones are ubiquitous and highly affordable today, making them an extremely efficient and accessible tool for recognizing dangerous driving patterns, especially in developing countries where expensive vehicle-integrated telemetry systems are not feasible. We use no hardware flush such as On-Board Diagnostics (OBD-II) dongles or expensive LiDAR arrays to provide a range of distinctive safety features accessible to all with smartphones.

However, most current Deep Learning (DL) models are unable to reason about the physical constraints of the vehicle's dynamic properties. Thus their predictions show high variance on urban driving patterns. Deep learning architectures currently excel at learning associations, but fundamentally fail at causalities, acting as a probabilistic "black box." This makes the system prone to "hallucinate" actions when the environment is noisy—like in the passenger compartment where a smartphone could fall off the dashboard or resonate with engine vibrations. These "hallucinations" reveal a critical Safety-Accuracy Gap: an AI might achieve high theoretical accuracy on clean data, but its lack of physical grounding makes it untrustworthy in the real world.

This paradox leads directly to the issue of "Environmental Unpredictability and Stochastic Noise," which is not just a theoretical problem but a fatal flaw in current data-driven systems. As highlighted by Shirole et al. (2025), standalone data-driven solutions possess fatal blind spots, as data-driven scoring remains sensitive to sensor placement and noise \cite{shirole2025}. Computer vision alternatives, while context-rich, carry computational complexity and privacy concerns prohibitive for edge-enabled devices with limited processing power \cite{qu2024}.

We introduce a "Physics-Constrained Post-Filtering" framework, deploying a \textbf{Probabilistic Soft Fusion} layer. Our core hypothesis is that while AI is superior for recognizing complex non-linear patterns, Newtonian Physics is required to define the \textit{boundary conditions} of reality. We propose a model-agnostic fusion layer that integrates domain-specific physical constraints at inference time. Rather than overriding the AI rigidly, our Soft Fusion acts as a gentle probabilistic prior, increasing confidence in physically consistent classes while strictly preserving probability calibration---a necessity for trustworthy edge deployment.

This work investigates whether integrating simple kinematic constraints improves the robustness of ensemble classifiers for smartphone-based maneuver recognition. We validate our approach with 12,000 examples derived from the CARLA simulator to model realistically noisy sensor readings. Our focus has been on evaluating three common bottlenecks: realistic simulation of corner cases, varying lateral G-force limits, and power limitations on the edge.

\textbf{Contributions}: The primary contributions of this work are as follows: a Probabilistic Soft Fusion layer acting as a "Safety Gate" that resolves the Safety-Accuracy Gap and preserves calibration; a 210-feature vector ensemble architecture optimized for the extreme latency constraints of edge telematics; a systematic comparative evaluation demonstrating that the base ensemble models degrade under environmental unpredictability and noise; and an empirical sensitivity analysis indicating optimal performance at $\alpha=0.1$.

\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:related}

This section synthesizes existing research to establish the theoretical foundation for the proposed Physics-Constrained Post-Filtering framework. The review is structured across four critical domains: first, we contextualize the urgency of the work within the Global Safety Crisis and the evolving paradigm of Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS). Second, we trace the methodological shift from heuristic thresholds to Hybrid Classifiers in Maneuver Recognition. Third, we examine the specific constraints and opportunities of Ensemble Learning within Edge Telematics environments. Finally, we explore the integration of physical safety constraints, which offers a mechanism to embed deterministic physical boundaries into probabilistic models, thereby addressing the reliability gaps identified in contemporary data-driven approaches.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Global Safety Crisis \& The ITS Context}
The need for advanced driver monitoring is clearly indicated by a recent WHO report that links traffic accidents to 1.19 million deaths annually, with the most severe impact in low-to-middle income countries \cite{who2023}. While traditionally Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) relied on an exterior architecture (inductive loops, cameras etc), the paradigm is shifting to "Inside-Out" telemetry. Shirole et al. (2025) point out challenges in driver behaviour scoring in many existing black-box driving scores where the physics of maneuver execution is not adequately considered \cite{shirole2025}.
 Furthermore, Qu et al. (2024) showed by benchmark-checking Computer Vision against Telemetry that although vision provides much more context, it is far too power hungry (battery drain and thermal throttling) for ubiquitous mobile sensor based monitoring compared to lightweight IMUs \cite{qu2024}.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Maneuver Recognition: The Move to Hybrid Classifiers}
From simple threshold based heuristic, the field of detecting risky driving incidents is moving towards full machine learning pipelines. A 2024 paper by Garefalakis et al. provides a strong baseline where Random Forest and XGBoost yielded 84\% accuracy for classification in mixed simulator trials \cite{garefalakis2024}. Shallow learning methods such as Random Forests were also shown to accurately extract time series signature of maneuvers without computational overhead from Deep Learning in \cite{xie2019}. Recent works show a move towards hybrid architectures where "Hybrid Models" are becoming the standard for classification with noise. Theodoraki et al. (2025) demonstrated that combining deep learning architectures (e.g., CNN-LSTM) with traditional machine learning techniques provides superior insights into naturalistic driving studies \cite{theodoraki2025}. This approach is also indicated by \cite{ijis2025} and \cite{ieee2025} where despite using supervised learning for automatic maneuver labelling, pure data-driven methods may have difficulty with rare edge cases.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Ensemble Learning in Edge Telematics}
Our choice of the UAH-based naturalistic dataset is motivated by its successful application in prior profiling studies. Ferreira et al. (2017) established the viability of smartphone-based profiling \cite{ferreira2017}, and Junior et al. (2017) explored Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) for temporal modeling \cite{junior2017}. However, while these foundational works established profiling baselines, they lacked the "Safety Gate" required to solve the contextual noise inherent in IMU data. We address this critical gap by transitioning from purely data-driven models to physics-fused systems.

In edge telematics, the trade-off between latency and accuracy is paramount. Although recent methods advocate lightweight Deep Learning models like ConvBiLSTMs \cite{mva2025} or GRUs \cite{ngc2025}, these often fail strict latency constraints. Conversely, simpler ensemble tree methods consistently outperform deep learning on tabular IMU data for edge deployment, offering superior robustness without the recursive overhead \cite{access2019, mdpi2024}. To handle the rare occurrence of emergencies, datasets must be augmented with synthetic SMOTE-based methods to clarify decision boundaries \cite{access2021}.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Physics-Constrained Post-Filtering}
A key component of this work is the evolution of Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PIML). While embedding physical equations directly as a regularizer during training is common \cite{karnakov2024}, applying physics post-classification as a \textit{Soft Prior} is strictly necessary for noisy smartphone IMUs. Rigid overrides invariably fail because sensor vibration causes transient, illusory physics violations. Our Probabilistic Soft Fusion framework integrates Newtonian limits as a post-classification prior. For Autonomous Driving, fusing noisy sensor data with mathematical motion models decreases prediction error \cite{karnakov2024}.
 Similarly, recent IEEE Sensors Letters (2025) research showed success using rule-based thresholds on GPS and gyroscope data \cite{sensorsletters2025}. Our Safety Layer extends this principle by calculating physical friction limits ($a_y \le \mu g$) as probabilistic priors to disqualify spurious AI-generated "hallucinations."

\section{Materials and Methods}\label{sec:methodology}

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Objectives}
The primary objective of this methodology is to develop a lightweight, model-agnostic physics layer capable of real-time operation on edge devices while maintaining analytical robustness against sensor noise.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Data Acquisition and Hybrid Domain Partitioning}
The model was trained on a fusion of GitHub Real-World Data and CARLA Simulator Data. To ensure absolute technical integrity, we utilized a "Vaulted Partitioning Strategy." The \textbf{Total Hybrid Training Pool} was divided into a 60/30/10 split: 60\% for training ensemble weights, 30\% for validation-driven hyperparameter tuning (specifically for selecting the optimal $\alpha$), and 10\% for internal consistency checks. 

Crucially, the \textbf{External Holdout} (comprising 40\% of the Mendeley and UAH-DriveSet naturalistic data) was completely sequestered in a "Data Vault." This external set was never seen by the model during training or hyperparameter selection, ensuring that the reported metrics reflect true cross-domain generalization.

\subsection{Sensor Pre-processing: Kalman Filtering and Feature Extraction}
Raw sensor data from mobile IMUs are notoriously susceptible to high-frequency road vibration and electromagnetic interference. To mitigate this, all telemetry streams ($a_x, a_y, \omega_z$) were processed through a single-dimensional \textbf{Kalman Filter} before statistical feature extraction. The filter was initialized with a process covariance ($Q = 1.0$) and a measurement covariance ($R = 1.0$), ensuring a balanced smoothing effect that prunes stochastic jitter while preserving maneuver-relevant kinematic transients.

\subsection{Evaluation Benchmarks: Kinematic Ground Truth (KGT)}
To evaluate "Theoretical Integrity" alongside real-world performance, we constructed a noise-free \textbf{Kinematic Ground Truth (KGT)} dataset (denoted as "Synthetic (Math)"). Unlike the high-fidelity simulator data which includes environmental noise, the KGT contains 100 ideal maneuver signatures generated purely from Newtonian equations (e.g., constant deceleration for \textit{Harsh Braking}). This serves as a "Control Environment" to measure AI hallucination rates in the absence of sensor noise.

\textbf{Environment Set-up}: We have used Town04 (Highway/High-Speed environment) for the generation of data associated with maneuvers that are risky in the physical world.

\end{itemize}
\textbf{Telemetry}: Vehicle velocity in real-time (km/h).
\textbf{Data Volume}: 91,421 samples were generated in total, covering five key maneuvers.

\subsection{Preprocessing (Sliding Windows + SMOTETomek)}

\FloatBarrier
\subsubsection{Signature Mapping \& Spectral Analysis}
We applied spectral density analysis to compare noise patterns in simulation with those occurring in reality.
\textbf{Time Series Synchronization}: Signals were aligned to obtain corresponding kinematic "signature".
\textbf{Spectral density (Welch Method)}: To assess noise frequency, we computed the spectral density using Welch's method. The analysis showed that the Real-World signals possess considerably more "high-frequency jitter" than simulated signals.

\FloatBarrier
\subsubsection{Feature extraction (Windowing Strategy)}
Standard ensemble models cannot process time series without processing. We transformed our time-series data into feature vectors by applying a sliding window technique (Algorithm \ref{alg:feature_extraction}):
\textbf{Window Size ($N=30$)}: A window of 30 samples, which translates to approximately 1.5 seconds at 20Hz, was applied in order to cover the entire kinematic "signature" of a maneuver.
\textbf{Step Size ($S=5$)}: A step of 5 samples was selected for smooth overlapping between consecutive windows.
\textbf{Feature Vector}: The windowed signals were flattened to form a continuous 210-length feature vector derived from the 30 samples across the 7 sensor channels, represented by $V=[a_{x,t0}, \dots, speed_{t29}]$. Each of these 210-feature vectors represents the unique kinematic "signature" for a given maneuver.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Feature Extraction \& Flattening}\label{alg:feature_extraction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Raw Sensor Stream $S \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times 7}$
\Ensure Feature Matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times 210}$
\For{$t = 0$ to $T-N$ step $S$}
    \State $Window \gets S[t : t+N]$
    \State $Vector \gets \text{Flatten}(Window)$ \Comment{Collapsing time dimension}
\State $X.\text{append}(Vector)$
\EndFor
\State \Return $X$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Combating the Class Imbalance Crisis \& Domain-Stratified Resampling}
As evidenced by the raw data distributions, the original datasets possessed an overwhelming bias toward the \texttt{Normal\_Driving} class. To prevent the ensemble models from developing a majority-class predictive bias, we implemented a rigorous "Domain-Stratified Balancing" protocol strictly isolated to the training phase.

First, to prevent cross-domain data corruption, the simulated (CARLA) and naturalistic (UAH-DriveSet) training partitions were processed independently. For each domain, the \texttt{Normal\_Driving} class was strategically downsampled by 80\% to reduce majority-class bias. Subsequently, the SMOTETomek algorithm was applied to synthesize samples for the under-represented maneuver classes while cleaning overlapping decision boundaries. After independent balancing, the domains were concatenated. 

The resulting training set of 65,438 samples represents the final balanced pool (approximately 13,000 per class), derived from the original 121,533 windows after downsampling and multi-domain stratification. Crucially, to prevent data leakage and preserve empirical validity, this resampling pipeline was strictly confined to the training dataset. The naturalistic holdout testing set ($N=12,045$) remains a strict 40\% stratified slice of the real-world dataset, completely excluded from any artificial balancing. By preserving the severe natural imbalance of the test set—where \texttt{Normal\_Driving} remains the vast majority—we ensure that the system's evaluation reflects the true stochastic reality of edge-deployed Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS), thereby providing an honest metric for its ability to prevent false alarms and "Alert Fatigue."

\subsection{Statistical Validation Protocol}
To rigorously evaluate the performance divergence between the standalone ensemble and the physics-fused framework, McNemar's test was employed. This non-parametric test is specifically suited for paired nominal data on a large holdout set ($N=12,045$), as it analyzes the discordant cells of the contingency table to determine if the improvement in maneuver recognition is statistically significant or due to random chance. 

The McNemar test statistic ($\chi^2$) is calculated based on the discordant pairs in the $2 \times 2$ contingency table:
$$\chi^2 = \frac{(|b - c| - 1)^2}{b + c}$$
Where:
$b$: The number of cases where the Base Ensemble was correct but the Hybrid Model was wrong.
$c$: The number of cases where the Base Ensemble was wrong but the Hybrid Model was correct.

The $-1$ in the numerator is the Yates's Continuity Correction, which makes the test more robust for real-world data.
\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Data-Driven Backbone Models}
Our architecture leverages four primary data-driven classification mechanisms: Random Forest (RF) acting as a bagging ensemble providing high precision and resistance to overfitting; XGBoost (XGB) and LightGBM (LGBM) as advanced gradient boosting frameworks that construct successive trees to minimize residual errors; and finally a Weighted Ensemble ($w_{RF}=2, w_{XGB}=1, w_{LGBM}=1$) that combines their probabilistic outputs to stabilize predictions. The Random Forest is double-weighted due to its higher precision in identifying safety-critical events. Crucially, these data-driven backbone models are trained entirely independently of physical constraints, where physics-guided adjustments are strictly reserved for inference.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Probabilistic Physics Fusion Layer (The Math)}
The core of the proposed framework is the integration of a kinematic safety layer at inference time. We define the fused probability vector $P_{new}$ as:
\begin{equation}
P_{new} = (1 - \alpha) P_{model} + \alpha P_{physics}
\end{equation}
where $P_{model}$ is the probabilistic output of the ensemble backbone, $P_{physics}$ is a rule-based one-hot constraint vector derived from Newtonian boundaries, and $\alpha \in [0,1]$ represents the fusion strength modulating the influence of the Newtonian boundaries. Empirical sensitivity analysis indicates optimal performance at $\alpha = 0.1$, allowing the physics to act as a gentle prior rather than a deterministic rigid override.

Thresholds for the $P_{physics}$ vector were explicitly derived from domain-specific vehicle dynamic limits, grounded in Civil Engineering principles (AASHTO guidelines) and the Coefficient of Friction ($\mu$) of standard asphalt. According to standard kinematic braking studies, a passenger vehicle on dry asphalt achieves a maximum sustainable longitudinal deceleration of $\sim -0.3g$ to $-0.4g$, while lateral friction boundaries dictate that standard cornering rarely exceeds $0.4g$ without losing traction. These physical boundaries prevent the AI from assigning high probabilities to actions that physically cannot occur without exceeding tire friction limits. This implementation is formally structured in Algorithm \ref{alg:physics_layer}.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Probabilistic Physics Fusion Layer}\label{alg:physics_layer}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Ensemble probability vector $P_{model}$, fusion weight $\alpha = 0.1$
\State \textit{Selection Note:} Empirical grid search on the 30\% validation set revealed that $\alpha=0.1$ provides the optimal balance.
\State \textbf{Initialize:} $P_{new} \gets P_{model}$
\If {$\min(a_x) < -3.0 m/s^2$} 
    \State $P_{physics} \gets [0, 1, 0, 0, 0]$ \Comment{Harsh Brake one-hot}
    \State $P_{new} \gets (1 - \alpha) P_{model} + \alpha P_{physics}$
\ElsIf {$\max(|a_y|) > 4.0 m/s^2$}
    \State $P_{physics} \gets [0, 0, 0, 1, 0]$ \Comment{Sharp Turn one-hot}
    \State $P_{new} \gets (1 - \alpha) P_{model} + \alpha P_{physics}$
\ElsIf {$\max(a_x) > 2.5 m/s^2$}
    \State $P_{physics} \gets [1, 0, 0, 0, 0]$ \Comment{Sudden Acceleration one-hot}
    \State $P_{new} \gets (1 - \alpha) P_{model} + \alpha P_{physics}$
\EndIf
\State \Return $\arg\max P_{new}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Reliability \& Calibration Metrics}
For the system's decision making to be reliable in safety-critical systems, a calibration of the predicted probabilities are tested; a perfectly calibrated model should give equal confidence as actual frequency of events.
\begin{equation}
P(Y=Y \mid P=p) = p
\end{equation}
This is measured using \textbf{Expected Calibration Error (ECE)}, where probabilities are grouped into $M$ bins:
\begin{equation}
ECE = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m|}{n} |\text{acc}(B_m) - \text{conf}(B_m)|
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of samples, $|B_m|$ is the number of samples in bin $m$, and $\text{acc}(B_m)$ and $\text{conf}(B_m)$ are the accuracy and confidence for bin respectively. Also tested for is the \textbf{Brier Score} to check mean squared difference of the predicted probability $f_i$ and the observed outcome $o_i$:
\begin{equation}
BS = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (f_i - o_i)^2
\end{equation}

\FloatBarrier
\subsubsection{Weighted Probability Fusion}
The confidence of the Ensemble's prediction ($P_{Final}$) is weighted averaged among the individual models to stabilize the prediction:
\begin{equation}
P_{Final} = \frac{2P_{RF} + 1P_{XGB} + 1P_{LGBM}}{4}
\end{equation}
Where $P_{RF}, P_{XGB}, P_{LGBM}$ are class-wise soft probability outputs for each respective classifier.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity and the Sim-to-Real Gap}
The 99.94\% recall on the Android benchmark represents a \textit{Sim-to-Real Generalization Collapse}. This near-perfect score suggests that the Ensemble model converged toward the specific spectral noise signatures and sensor sampling biases inherent to specific mobile hardware. Rather than reflecting true maneuver generalization, this value serves as a control baseline. It proves that purely data-driven models are "Expert Interpreters" of their specific domain but lack the robustness required for unknown environmental conditions. 

The Hybrid model’s shift to 90.62\% on this same set is a critical safety feature; the Physics Layer prunes stochastic spikes that the AI mistakes for maneuvers, effectively trading "overconfident recall" for "kinematic plausibility." By integrating the Newtonian Kinematic Layer, we successfully regularized this convergence. The Physics Layer serves as a universal anchor, evidenced by the significant recall recovery in simulator environments ($28.6\% \to 74.7\%$ in CARLA). This "Hybrid Victory" proves that the kinematics layer acts as a "Generalization Safety Net," resolving the conflict between \textbf{Stochastic Noise vs. Kinematic Truth}.

\subsection{Performance Limitations and Safety Bias}
While the Hybrid architecture significantly improves reliability in unseen domains, a targeted analysis of the confusion metrics reveals a specific limitation in \textit{Sudden Acceleration} detection (see Figure \ref{fig:recall_heatmap}). Acceleration events often manifest with lower G-force magnitudes compared to Braking maneuvers. The cascaded effect of Kalman smoothing ($\lambda=0.1$ as defined in Section 3.3) and the strict Longitudinal prior may cause the "Safety Gate" to misidentify these low-magnitude maneuvers as Normal Driving. This constitutes a "Safety Bias" where the system prioritizes the elimination of false alarms over high-resolution detection of subtle longitudinal changes.

\subsection{Probability Calibration and Precision Integrity}
A critical achievement of the Hybrid model is the improvement in probability calibration. As shown in Table \ref{tab:calibration_metrics}, the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) dropped from 0.142 to 0.041, indicating that the Soft Fusion mechanism aligns model confidence with actual physical reality. This prevents "Alert Fatigue" in real-world telematics deployments by ensuring that the system only triggers when physical thresholds are consistently exceeded.
Physics thresholds are heuristic but data-informed. The fusion weight $\alpha$ was selected via validation, meaning optimal calibration may shift under different environmental conditions. Furthermore, there is no real-time deployment evaluation in this specific iteration. Future work will benchmark live latencies in embedded devices.

In contrast, the proposed Physics-Constrained Post-Filtering framework introduces a deterministic Safety Layer derived from AASHTO standards, achieves verified statistical stability through a 10-run ensemble analysis ($\sigma = \pm0.0054$), and confirms 0.06ms inference latency on Intel i7 / RTX 4050 hardware (projected $<$15ms on ARMv8). This positions our work as a framework that demonstrates practical feasibility and improves stability under noise while respecting the physical and computational constraints of consumer-grade Intelligent Transportation Systems with a Macro Precision of 85.01\%.

\FloatBarrier
\subsection{Why Rigid Physics Fails}
The empirical degradation observed under rigid physics enforcement ($\alpha \ge 0.5$) highlights strict theoretical limitations of deterministic logic in edge telematics. The failure mechanism is multifold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Posterior Distortion:} Rigid thresholds forcefully truncate the probability distribution, effectively collapsing the ensemble's sophisticated uncertainty calibration and transforming a probabilistic prediction into a binary dictate.
    \item \textbf{Noise Amplification:} Unfiltered high-frequency chassis vibrations momentarily breach kinematic thresholds (e.g., a momentary $a_y$ spike from a pothole). A strict override blindly amplifies this mechanical noise into a false positive maneuver alert.
    \item \textbf{Hard Prior Mismatch:} A rigid threshold assumes the physical boundary is an absolute truth uncorrupted by sensor error. In reality, consumer-grade IMUs exhibit stochastic jitter, meaning the "hard prior" is fundamentally mismatched with the low-fidelity data stream.
    \item \textbf{Class Imbalance Sensitivity:} Because emergency maneuvers are exceptionally rare relative to Normal Driving, rigid force-triggers overwhelmingly occur during safe driving states, cascading into systemic "Alert Fatigue" and destroying user trust.
\end{itemize}
By utilizing a soft prior ($\alpha=0.1$), the Probabilistic Soft Fusion architecture successfully circumvents these failure modes, demonstrating that continuous probability modulation is structurally superior to discrete logic gates on noisy sensor arrays.

\FloatBarrier
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
This study presented a probabilistic physics-guided ensemble architecture for edge-based maneuver recognition. Our primary finding is that while rigid physics-based logic gates can theoretically eliminate false positives in clean synthetic data, they become liabilities in noisy real-world environments. The deterministic "Safety Override" mechanism inadvertently rejected valid maneuvers due to vibration noise. By shifting to a probabilistic physics-guided fusion layer ($\alpha=0.1$), we resolved this conflict. With a Macro Precision of 0.8165 and an F1-score of 0.7224, along with verified statistical stability ($\sigma=0.0050$), the proposed framework offers a mathematically robust balance of sensitivity and physical grounding. 

Our results confirm that AI is the pattern engine, while Physics is the safety boundary. By blending them at a weight of $\alpha=0.1$, we achieve a system that is 833x faster than real-time requirements and provides statistically significant gains in classification integrity ($p < 0.001$). Future work will focus on integrating camera feeds with IMU data and optimizing the ensemble for ARM microcontrollers.
